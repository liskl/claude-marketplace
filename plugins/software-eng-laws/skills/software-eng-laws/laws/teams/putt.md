# Putt's Law

**Category**: teams
**Detection**: manual
**Short description**: Technology is dominated by two types: those who understand what they don't manage, and those who manage what they don't understand.

## Overview

![Putt's Law](../images/putts-law.png)

Putt's Law explains a known concept: that the best engineers aren't in charge, and those in charge aren't deeply knowledgeable about the technology. The corollary describes a "competence inversion" where upper management knows less than subordinates. This disconnect produces dysfunction across organizations, as managers set unrealistic deadlines while engineers build solutions misaligned with business needs.

## Takeaways

- There exists a frequent gap between technical expertise and management positions, with few individuals excelling at both.
- Technical experts typically remain outside management while managers lack deep technical knowledge.
- Misalignment causes problems, including unrealistic expectations and frustrated teams.
- Successful organizations address this through manager training or promoting technically-skilled leaders.

## Examples

A sales-background manager pushes rapid feature development without understanding quality considerations, contrasted with a skilled sysadmin excluded from budget decisions. A typical scenario involves managers promising clients unrealistic timelines, frustrating engineering teams unaware of technical complexity.

## Signals

## Scoring Rubric
- ⚪ **Manual**: reflect on the prompts below.

## Reflection Prompts
- Do technical decisions get made by people who actually read the code?
- How often does management override an engineering recommendation against the engineers' advice?
- Are your most senior ICs still coding, or have they been absorbed into management tracks?

## Remediation Hints
- Maintain a dual career track (IC / management) with equal pay and prestige at senior levels.
- Require architectural decisions to include a sign-off from an active hands-on engineer.

## Origins

Putt's Law was revealed in the satirical book *Putt's Law and the Successful Technocrat* (1981). The author adopted the pseudonym "Archibald Putt" while providing cynical insights into technical organization dynamics and hierarchical incompetence patterns.
